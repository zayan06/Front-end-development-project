1. Arrow Signals: Protected vs. Permissive

Turning at an intersection is one of the most dangerous maneuvers. Arrow signals dictate exactly who has the right of way.

⬅️

Solid Green Arrow

Protected Turn: You have the absolute right of way. All oncoming traffic and crossing pedestrians are facing a red light.

🟡

Flashing Yellow Arrow

Permissive Turn: You may turn, but you must yield to oncoming traffic and pedestrians first. Proceed only when safe.

🟥

Solid Red Arrow

No Turning: You may not turn in the direction of the arrow, even if the main circular light is green. You must wait for the arrow to turn green or flashing yellow.

2. How Intersections "See" Cars

Traffic lights aren't just on dumb timers anymore. They use advanced sensors to detect waiting vehicles and adjust the lights dynamically.

Induction Loops

These are the circular or rectangular cuts you see in the asphalt just behind the white stop line. They contain coils of wire. When a heavy metal object (like your car) drives over them, it changes the magnetic field, telling the traffic computer you are waiting.


Pro Tip: To trigger the light, make sure your car's front tires pull up completely to the thick white limit line!

Video & Radar Detection

Look at the poles holding the traffic lights. If you see small cameras or white domed boxes pointing down at the lanes, the intersection uses video or radar to detect movement and vehicle presence. These are often used instead of induction loops because they don't require tearing up the road.

3. Emergency Vehicle Preemption

Have you ever noticed a tiny white flashing light near a traffic signal as a fire truck approaches? This is an Opticom system.

Emergency vehicles emit a specific strobe light or infrared signal. The sensor on the traffic light pole detects it and immediately forces the intersection to safely turn green for the emergency vehicle, while turning all other directions red.

4. Pedestrian Signals & HAWK Beacons

Standard intersections use the "Walk" and "Don't Walk" signs, but mid-block crossings often use a specialized system called a HAWK Beacon (High-Intensity Activated crossWalK).

Dark (Off)

Proceed normally. No pedestrians are present.

Flashing Yellow

A pedestrian has pushed the button. Prepare to stop.

Solid Red

Complete stop. Pedestrians are crossing.

Flashing Red

Treat as a STOP sign. Stop, and if the crosswalk is completely clear, you may proceed.

5. Transit & Bicycle Signals

In modern cities, standard vehicles aren't the only ones getting specific instructions.

Light Rail & Bus Signals

Transit vehicles often use signals with solid white lines instead of red, yellow, and green circles. A horizontal white line means "Stop," a vertical white line means "Go," and a diagonal line means "Prepare to Stop/Turn."

Bicycle Signals

Look like standard traffic lights but feature a bicycle icon inside the lens. These protect cyclists by giving them a head start before cars are allowed to turn right.
